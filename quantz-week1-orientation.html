Core Pillars of Quantitative Finance

üö´ No-Arbitrage Principle

The foundation of modern finance theory: in efficient markets, risk-free profit opportunities cannot persist.

Historical Roots

Developed from financial economics in the 1970s, the no-arbitrage principle states that identical assets must trade at identical prices, adjusted for risk and time.

Why Arbitrage Vanishes

  • Market participants quickly identify price discrepancies
  • Automated trading systems execute arbitrage strategies instantly
  • Capital flows eliminate price differences within milliseconds

Interactive Arbitrage Detector

‚öñÔ∏è Risk-Neutral Pricing

The mathematical framework that enables derivative pricing by transforming real-world probabilities into risk-neutral measures.

Intuition Before Math

Risk-neutral pricing doesn't assume investors are risk-neutral. Instead, it's a mathematical technique that simplifies pricing by adjusting probabilities to account for risk preferences.

Fundamental Pricing Formula

Price = Expected payoff under risk-neutral measure, discounted at risk-free rate

üìà Stochastic Processes Primer

The mathematical foundation for modeling random price movements in financial markets.

Brownian Motion

Continuous-time random process with independent, normally distributed increments.

Martingales

Processes where the expected future value equals the current value - no predictable drift.

Random Walks

Discrete-time processes where each step is independent and identically distributed.

Case Studies & Historical Anecdotes

üìö Famous Arbitrage Cases

Learning from history: when theory meets practice and risk management.

üèõÔ∏è Long-Term Capital Management (1998)

The Strategy: Convergence trading and relative value arbitrage

The Theory: Mean reversion in bond spreads and statistical arbitrage

The Reality: Extreme leverage (25:1) and correlated risks during crisis

The Lesson: Model risk, liquidity risk, and the importance of stress testing

üí± Covered Interest Parity Breakdown

The Theory: Interest rate differentials should equal forward premiums

The Breakdown: Post-2008 regulatory changes and bank funding costs

The Opportunity: Persistent deviations in FX forward markets

The Lesson: Regulatory arbitrage and structural market changes

‚ö° Flash Crash (2010)

The Event: 1000-point Dow drop in minutes

The Cause: Algorithmic trading and liquidity evaporation

The Impact: Circuit breakers and market structure reforms

The Lesson: Systemic risk from automated trading systems
